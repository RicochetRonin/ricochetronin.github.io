PPJ 09

What went right:

Our team was initially uneven in terms of skill sets, we had a lot of members with development experience and not a lot with design experience. Despite this bias, however, I think that the members who tried design work really stepped up to the plate and put out some solid work.
I think the development team was generally in sync with each other. We had minimal issues and conflicts whenever we merged our work for the week.
Despite not completing all the goals we had initially set out to complete, we were able to effectively scope down and still have a solid vertical slice to showcase.

What went wrong:

At one point, we had more design team members start implementing their own work on our development branch. There were some issues merging branches, as some of my implementations were overwritten by another. This took a few hours to fix which could’ve been better spent elsewhere.
I’d say our team generally did things later than we should have. This applies to creating PPJ entries, creating new builds, and collecting playtest data. While we were able to submit most of our deliverables on time, I feel that some haven’t had the best quality.
We were able to implement a lot of enemies, abilities, and VFX into our game, however, everything may not have been implemented in the best way. For example, the impact animations that play whenever a bullet hits a wall are game objects that are instantiated and destroyed. This isn’t optimized, as they should use object pooling. There’s a few things like this that could be better optimized but weren’t due to lack of time.

Lessons Learned Going Forward:

Professor Diefenbach mentioned that dev and design should work together closely. Each team shouldn’t just work on their own work and combine it in the end. They should work together from the start so they know what to expect from the other in the end. Looking back on some of the assets we created, I think they would’ve been easier to create had I known what to expect from the design team. Going forward, I plan to better follow Professor Diefenbach’s method of asset creating, reaching out to the design team early on.
